1.0 Background

The brake torque of an engine usually drops at high speed due to challenges in getting sufficient air into the cylinder. Since intake manifold is the last step before the air-fuel mixture enters the cylinder, we would like to conduct a self-guided study to determine how the engine performance is improved by manifold tuning.

2.0 Manifold Overview

An intake manifold is defined as a part of an engine that supplies the fuel/air mixture to the cylinders. [1]As shown in Figure 1, the ambient air enters the air intake, and the throttle body mainly controls the amount of the air entering manifold. The upstream part of the manifold before the air gets distributed is called the manifold plenum, and the individual ducts downstream are called manifold runners. Differences in Plenum and runner design can bring influences on engine performance. For instance, A high-performance manifold typically has large plenum and oversized runners to allow a large mass flow of air into the cylinder. Additionally, the minor loss of air flow can be reduced by having fewer bends and matching the size of runner inlet and cylinder head.

One of the purposes of manifold tuning is to improve volumetric efficiency. As shown in equation 1,

The volumetric efficiency is the ratio of actual air inducted to the theoretical amount of the air.  Therefore, for the same amount of theoretical air, the more air enters the cylinder, the higher volumetric efficiency. To increase volumetric efficiency, it is necessary to consider pressure wave phenomena. Figure 2 shows the generation of expansion and compression pressure waves generated due to the open and close of the intake valve. Shortly after the inlet valve of cylinder opens, the pressure in the cylinder drops and an expansion (associated with a decrease in pressure) wave starts traveling towards the atmosphere end of the manifold. The traveling speed of the wave is a-v, where a is the isentropic speed of sound while v is the fluid velocity in the manifold. When the wave reaches the open end of the pipe, the expansion wave is reflected as a compression wave with a speed of a+v. If the manifold is well designed, the inlet valve will be closing when the compression wave arrives, and the compression wave will increase pressure. [2]

4.0 Variable-Length Intake Manifold

As covered in the previous section that longer runners are preferred for low engine speed and shorter runners are preferred for high engine speed, some engines use variable-length intake manifold to allow high volumetric efficiency at both high and low engine speed. Figure 4 displays a folded manifold with a valve. At low engine speed, the valve is switched to the position that intake air is guided through the longer pipe; At high engine speed, the valve is switched to another position that guides intake air through the shorter pipe.

6.0 Conclusion          

The manifold tuning advances engine performance by increasing the air intake into the cylinder and the higher air intake enhance the volumetric efficiency of an engine. The popular approaches for manifold tuning include using organ pipes, variable length intake manifold, and Helmholtz resonator.
